Learning Objective 01

Probability as a foundation of statistical modeling, including inference and maximum likelihood estimation

Statistical modeling is not just curve-fitting — it is the act of making probability statements about the world given data. This objective asks whether I understand that foundation: what a p-value means, what a confidence interval commits us to, and how maximum likelihood connects a model’s distributional assumptions to the estimates we compute.

Show: Homework I — Scatter Plot and Regression Line

Evidence · Homework-I.Rmd — Q1b (Auto dataset)

Part (b) asked to plot mpg ~ horsepower with the least-squares regression line. The fitted model was mpĝ = 39.9 − 0.158 × horsepower, with R² = 0.606 and p < 0.001 for the slope.

Fig 1 · mpg ~ horsepower (Auto dataset, n=392). Negative linear relationship: each unit increase in horsepower is associated with a 0.158 decrease in mpg. The large p-value for the slope (<2e−81) is a direct probability statement under H₀: β₁ = 0.

Show: Homework I — Residual Diagnostics

Evidence · Homework-I.Rmd — Q1c (residuals vs fitted)

Part (c) required checking the linear regression assumptions by plotting residuals against fitted values and examining the Q-Q plot.

Fig 2 · Residuals vs. Fitted for mpg ~ horsepower. The LOESS smooth (green) is not flat — it curves, indicating the linear model misses systematic structure. The spread also fans out at higher fitted values (heteroscedasticity). Both violations mean the probabilistic basis for inference does not hold here.

Tell: Why this demonstrates the objective

Maximum likelihood estimation underlies OLS when we assume normally distributed errors: the OLS estimates are exactly the MLEs of β under that model. Every p-value I computed flows from this assumption — the t-statistics follow a t-distribution because of the normality assumption on ε. When I diagnosed the curved residual pattern and heteroscedasticity in the Boston dataset, I ran a Shapiro-Wilk test and formally rejected the normality assumption, concluding the model should not be used as-is. That willingness to reject the probabilistic foundation when the evidence demands it is, I think, the clearest sign that I understand what probability is doing here — it is not decoration, it is the engine of inference.

Supporting evidence · Homework-I, Q2

In the bias-variance question, I explained why a cubic model has lower training RSS but higher expected test RSS when the true relationship is linear — reasoning entirely in terms of expectations under a probability model.

Learning Objective 02

Apply the appropriate generalized linear model for a specific data context

Choosing the right model means matching the model’s assumptions to what the data actually are. This objective is best demonstrated through Project 2, where we faced three distinct outcome types and had to make principled choices for each — including replacing a model that failed its own assumptions.

Show: Project 2 — Poisson → Negative Binomial

Evidence · project2_classification_k12.Rmd — Section 8

FSFREQ (times parents attended school activities) is a non-negative integer count, making Poisson regression the natural starting point. Before interpreting any results, we checked the core Poisson assumption: variance equals the mean.

Mean (FSFREQ)7.02
Variance (FSFREQ)85.4
Var / Mean12.2×
Poisson deviance ratio5.12

Fig 3 · Why Poisson fails on FSFREQ (Project 2). Left: what Poisson assumes — a symmetric distribution where variance equals the mean (ratio = 1.0). Right: the actual FSFREQ distribution, heavily right-skewed with variance 12.2× larger than the mean. Using Poisson here would severely underestimate standard errors.

A ratio of 12.2 is severe overdispersion. Proceeding with Poisson would produce standard errors that are too small, making predictors appear significant when they are not. We moved to Negative Binomial regression, confirmed the improvement with a likelihood ratio test (LR = 60.3, p < 0.001), and noted the AIC improved by approximately 35,000 points.

# Poisson deviance/df = 5.12 >> 1: invalid, standard errors underestimated # Var/mean ratio from EDA = 12.2: confirms severe overdispersion # Negative Binomial adds dispersion parameter θ nb_mod <- poisson_reg() |> set_engine("glm.nb") |> set_mode("regression") # LR test NB vs Poisson: statistic=60.3, p<0.001 — NB wins

Show: Homework IV — Multinomial Logistic Regression

Evidence · Homework-IV.Rmd (nonvoters dataset, n=5,836)

Fitting multinomial logistic regression for voter_category ∈ {rarely/never, sporadic, always}. I extended the class model by adding party identification, compared the two models, and interpreted coefficients in both log-odds and odds-ratio form.

Fig 4 · Voter category by party (HW-IV, ggbivariate()). The “Other” group has ~50% rarely/never voters vs. ~22% for Democrats and Republicans — confirming party adds meaningful predictive information beyond demographics alone.

Democrats had 4.06× higher odds of being an “always” voter vs. “rarely/never” compared to “Other” identifiers (β = 1.401). Republicans showed 3.45× higher odds (β = 1.239). Adding party reduced the gender coefficient from −0.211 to −0.192, suggesting some of the gender–voting relationship is mediated by party identity.

Supporting: Project 2 — LDA as a Theoretically Distinct Approach

We also applied Linear Discriminant Analysis alongside logistic regression. Where logistic regression models P(Y=k|X) directly, LDA models P(X|Y=k) and uses Bayes’ theorem. LDA achieved AUC = 0.651 vs. logistic regression’s 0.743 — a gap that was principled and expected: LDA could not use the categorical predictors (race, parent education, disability) that drove logistic regression’s performance. Comparing the two taught me that “appropriate model” depends on the nature of predictors available, not just the outcome type.

Key insight: Choosing the right GLM means diagnosing when your first choice fails its own assumptions (Poisson → NB), and understanding why two valid methods produce different results by design (logistic vs. LDA).
Learning Objective 03

Demonstrate model selection given a set of candidate models

Model selection is the process of choosing from a set of candidates honestly — not overfitting to the data we happen to have, and not using the same data to both select and evaluate. I have demonstrated this through best-subset selection in Project 1 and manual backward elimination in HW-III.

Show: Project 1 — Non-linearity as Motivation for Polynomial Terms

Evidence · project1_diamonds_linear_regression.Rmd — Section 2.3 (nonlinear-check chunk)

Before any model selection, we established that the relationship between physical dimension x and carat weight is non-linear. The Rmd code overlays both a linear fit and a LOESS smooth on the scatter of carat vs. x.

Fig 5 · Carat vs. x (length in mm) from Project 1 EDA. The LOESS smooth (green) clearly curves above the linear fit (red dashed) — confirming that poly(x, 2) is warranted and that a purely linear candidate set would be mis-specified from the start.

Show: Project 1 — Best-Subset Selection with Held-Out Validation

Evidence · project1_diamonds_linear_regression.Rmd — Section 4 (validation-set chunk)

With 25 candidate predictors we used exhaustive best-subset selection via regsubsets(). The critical methodological decision was to compute validation-set MSE for each model size using a three-way train/validation/test split — the test set was never touched until the very end.

# Three-way holdout — data leakage prevention diamonds_split <- initial_split(diamonds_clean, prop = 0.80) train_val <- training(diamonds_split) # 80% test_set <- testing(diamonds_split) # 20% — never touched until end val_split <- initial_split(train_val, prop = 0.80) train_set <- training(val_split) # 64% — model fitting val_set <- testing(val_split) # 16% — model selection

Fig 6 · Validation MSE by number of predictors (Project 1). Minimized at k=5 (red point). The sharp spike at k=7→8 — when quality-grade categorical variables first enter — exposes multicollinearity that in-sample criteria like AIC would miss entirely.

Learning Objective 04

Express the results of statistical models to a general audience

Numbers from a model mean nothing until they are explained to someone who did not fit it. This objective is about translating statistical outputs into language a non-statistician can act on.

Show: Homework II — Interaction Term Motivation and Interpretation

Evidence · Homework-II.Rmd — Interaction exploration (Carseats dataset)

Before including a Price × ShelveLoc interaction, the Rmd code plots Sales vs. Price separately for each level of ShelveLoc using gf_point() |> gf_lm(). The non-parallel slopes are the visual evidence that the interaction is warranted.

Fig 7 · Sales vs. Price by ShelveLoc (HW-II). Good shelving (green) has a consistently higher intercept and a distinctly different slope than Bad (red) — the non-parallel lines are precisely what justifies the Price × ShelveLoc interaction term.

Plain-language interpretation (Homework-II)

“Keeping everything else constant, when carseats are placed on a medium-quality shelf as opposed to a bad shelf, the sales volume decreases by (62 − 6) = 56 units for every $1 increase in price.”

A retailer does not want to know β1 and β7 separately — they want to know: if I raise price by $1 on a medium shelf, what happens to sales? Combining main effects and interaction terms into a single actionable sentence is what communication to a general audience requires.

Show: Project 1 — The Jeweler’s Insight

In the Project 1 report, we translated a test RMSE of 0.026 carats into a tangible implication: “A jeweler equipped with a millimeter gauge and a table-angle tool could estimate any diamond’s carat weight to within roughly 0.026 carats, without a scale.” We also made the counterintuitive finding legible: quality grades (cut, color, clarity) — the features most buyers focus on — add no predictive power once physical dimensions are known, because large diamonds are difficult to cut and clarity-grade perfectly.

Tell: Growth Over the Semester

In HW-I my interpretations were correct but mechanical: “The relationship is negative as indicated by the negative slope.” By Project 2, I was writing: “Racial and ethnic disparities persist after controlling for income and parent education, suggesting structural factors beyond socioeconomic status.” The habit of pairing every model output with a plain-language sentence has become automatic.

Learning Objective 05

Use programming software to fit and assess statistical models

TidyModels provides a unified interface for fitting, assessing, and comparing models. This objective is about fluency with that interface — not just running code, but understanding the workflow and making deliberate implementation choices.

Show: Homework II — Full TidyModels Pipeline

# 1. Specification lm_spec <- linear_reg() |> set_engine("lm") |> set_mode("regression") # 2. Split + fit with interaction carseats_split <- initial_split(carseats, prop = 0.80) model <- lm_spec |> fit(Sales ~ Price * ShelveLoc + Age + Advertising, data = training(carseats_split)) # 3. Assess tidy(model) # coefficients, SEs, p-values glance(model) # R², adj R², AIC, BIC augment(model, new_data = testing(carseats_split)) # Test R² = 0.726 vs train R² = 0.675 → generalises well

Show: Homework III — Reusable Function

# Encapsulate the repetitive step-reporting pattern report_step <- function(model, step_title = "Step 1", alpha = 0.05) { td <- tidy(model) gl <- glance(model) candidates <- td |> filter(term != "(Intercept)", p.value > alpha) |> arrange(desc(p.value)) print(step_title); print(td); print(gl); print(candidates) }

Show: Project 2 — Advanced TidyModels

Project 2 pushed our software use into more advanced territory. To address the 31:1 class imbalance in grade categories, we used step_upsample() from the {themis} package — applied only within training folds so the test set remained in its natural distribution. We also applied repair_call(), a TidyModels utility that fixes an internal reference bug preventing augment() from working on fitted multinomial models.

For model validation we used 10-fold cross-validation via vfold_cv() and fit_resamples(). The CV AUC of 0.726 closely tracking the test AUC of 0.743 confirmed that the held-out result was not a lucky split. Everything was bundled into workflow() objects that combine the recipe preprocessing steps with the model specification, keeping the pipeline auditable and preventing any data leakage between folds.

Reflection: My background as an ML engineer meant I was comfortable with Python and sklearn. Learning TidyModels taught me a different philosophy — where each step is explicit and auditable. I now apply this pipeline thinking back in my ML work.
